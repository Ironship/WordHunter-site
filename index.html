A reading-to-memory workflow

Do not just collect words. Build a path back to them.

A personal word base is useful when it changes what happens after you close a book. Word Hunter keeps each useful word connected to its sentence, then turns it into an active-recall prompt and schedules another encounter instead of letting it disappear into a notebook.

  1. 01
    Read something you care about

    Use a book, article, subtitle file, EPUB, or PDF. Meaningful context gives an unknown word a situation, tone, and reason to remember it.

  2. 02
    Save the words worth learning

    Add a translation, grammatical article, note, image, and the original sentence. Your database becomes a record of your vocabulary, not somebody else's generic list.

  3. 03
    Try to recall before revealing

    Reader prompts and flashcards ask for the meaning first. Reveal the answer, use the context as feedback, and rate how well you remembered.

  4. 04
    Return when the word is due

    The SRS queue spaces later reviews. You spend time on words that need attention while better-remembered words wait longer between reviews.

Personal word base

Turn reading history into learning material

Search, filter, edit, export, and revisit words from your own texts. The source sentence remains available as a richer cue than a translation alone.

Active recall

Practice producing the answer

Looking at a translation can feel familiar without proving that you can retrieve it. A recall attempt makes the review diagnostic and gives feedback something to correct.

Spaced repetition

Review less often, but more deliberately

Due dates distribute practice over time. Word Hunter also waits until at least the next day before a newly learned word becomes an in-reader recall prompt.

What the research says

The features support well-studied learning habits

These papers study learning methods, not Word Hunter itself. They explain why context, retrieval, repeated encounters, and spacing are useful ingredients to combine.

Use the evidence, not the hype. Saving every unfamiliar word is not automatically effective. Choose comprehensible material, keep the words that matter to you, attempt recall before checking the answer, and treat context as feedback. Results still depend on attention, prior knowledge, text difficulty, review quality, and repeated exposure.
